Labour Unpaid Wages Basic Information

This document summarizes official-source guidance for workers facing non-payment of wages, delayed salary, withheld final settlement, unpaid internship stipend, unpaid weekly payment, unpaid delivery worker payout, unpaid contractor payment, or related labour grievance issues.

Common labour dispute situations include:
- employer has not paid salary
- company is holding salary
- internship stipend is pending
- weekly wage not received
- delivery boy payment not received
- final settlement is pending
- wages are delayed
- employer is refusing payment after work
- contractor or company is not paying for completed work

A worker facing unpaid wages, delayed salary, or unpaid stipend should first collect basic evidence such as employment proof, offer letter, internship letter, appointment letter if available, salary slips, bank statements, attendance records, work messages, identity proof, employer details, workplace address, and written communication about unpaid wages.

Depending on the nature of employment and jurisdiction, the worker may approach the appropriate labour authority, labour commissioner, or relevant labour grievance portal. In central-sector matters, the Chief Labour Commissioner Central and Ministry of Labour and Employment mechanisms may be relevant. State labour departments may handle state-level establishments and local labour disputes.

For online grievance filing, the worker should provide clear details such as employer name, workplace address, period of employment, amount pending, type of payment pending, dates of non-payment, and supporting documents.

For unpaid salary, wages, final settlement, weekly payment, or internship stipend-related issues, the user should avoid relying only on verbal communication. They should keep copies of written requests, emails, messages, salary proof, attendance proof, work proof, and payment records.

If the worker cannot afford legal representation, they may also seek legal aid through the appropriate Legal Services Authority.

NyayaSetu should not decide the legal merits of the labour claim. It should guide the user to collect documents, identify the appropriate labour authority, use official grievance mechanisms, and seek legal aid if required.
